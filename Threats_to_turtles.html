Threats to Turtles in Sri Lanka

Sri Lankan turtles, ancient reptiles that have inhabited Earth's oceans for millions of years, confront a multitude of challenges threatening their survival. Natural risks include predation by monitor lizards, canines, and seagulls, as well as diseases and parasites. Natural disasters like cyclones and tsunamis devastate nesting grounds, while climate change impacts such as rising temperatures and sea levels disrupt nesting areas and affect hatchling sex ratios. Changes in oceanic factors and prey availability further hinder turtle foraging and survival. Human-induced threats, such as habitat deterioration, illegal wildlife trade, egg harvesting, bycatch in fishing nets, pollution, and predation, also pose significant dangers. Coastal development, tourism, pollution, and climate change exacerbate habitat degradation, reducing nesting locations and disrupting feeding ecosystems.

Water Polution

Water pollution in Sri Lankan Sea bodies has a significant impact on sea turtles, posing serious dangers to their survival and well-being. The release of untreated sewage, industrial waste, and agricultural runoff into the sea brings dangerous chemicals and pollutants into the aquatic habitats of turtles, causing serious health problems like as organ damage, hormonal abnormalities, and immune system limitations. Additionally, heavy metals and other harmful elements in the water can induce abnormalities and deformities in sea turtle hatchlings, decreasing their chances of survival. Water pollution also affects the delicate balance of marine ecosystems, depleting critical prey species, and inducing plastic trash. It is critical to develop effective strategies to limit the effects of water pollution on sea turtles in Sri Lanka, such as enacting tough restrictions and enforcing them to avoid the discharge of untreated sewage and industrial waste, encouraging appropriate waste management, recycling, and limiting single-use plastics, and educating local residents, fishermen, and tourists on the necessity of maintaining clean and healthy maritime habitats.

Solutions

Habitat Loss and Degradation

Sea turtles in Sri Lanka are facing serious threats due to the degradation of nesting beaches and foraging areas caused by coastal development, sand mining, and coastal erosion. These activities reduce the availability of nesting sites, lowering the number of eggs placed and hatchlings produced, and can lead to malnutrition, lower growth rates, and compromised immune systems. Additionally, coastal development interferes with the natural coastal processes on which sea turtles rely, making it difficult for turtles to access good nesting places and reducing their chances of survival. Conservation activities, such as marine protected areas, habitat conservation efforts, and sustainable coastal development practices, can help offset negative impacts and preserve the long-term survival of sea turtles in Sri Lanka.

Solutions

Illeagal Harvesting, Trading and Fishing Industry

Sea turtles in Sri Lanka face challenges from unlawful harvesting and trading, as well as fishing operations, which further deplete their populations. Poachers hunt these creatures for their meat, eggs, and shells, fueled by traditional beliefs and cultural customs. Incidental capture of sea turtles is also a major hazard to them, as nets, lines, and traps used in fishing can entangle or drown turtles, causing damage or death. To combat this, it is critical to strengthen law enforcement, establish strong punishments, and raise awareness about the necessity of safeguarding sea turtles. Mitigation strategies such as the incorporation of turtle excluder devices (TEDs) into fishing gear can drastically reduce turtle bycatch. Promoting sustainable fishing techniques, assisting communities reliant on sea turtle exploitation, and encouraging collaboration between fishermen and conservation organizations are all critical measures for safeguarding sea turtles in Sri Lanka's waters.
